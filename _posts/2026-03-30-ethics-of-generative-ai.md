---
Title: "The Admission Machine: Can an Algorithm Hear My Voice?"
Date: 2026-03-30
Author: "Ritvik Diada"
---

Last night, as I was proofreading an essay for a summer program, I caught myself wondering who—or what—would actually be on the other side of the "Submit" button. We talk a lot about AI in the classroom, but we rarely talk about the "Admission Machine." In the race to process tens of thousands of applications, many institutions are turning to Large Language Models (LLMs) to scan for sentiment, keywords, and "fit."

While AI offers a quick fix for overwhelmed offices, I have to ask a difficult question. Does handing the evaluation of human potential over to an algorithm strip away the very nuance that makes us individuals?

### The Tension Between Efficiency and Empathy
Admissions officers are human. They get tired, they have personal biases, and they have very little time. On paper, AI seems like the perfect way to ensure every essay gets a fast and "objective" initial screening. 

However, AI is fundamentally a rearview mirror. It is trained on data from *past* successful applications. According to the [White House Blueprint for an AI Bill of Rights](https://www.whitehouse.gov), automated systems can deeply embed past societal biases into new decisions. If an algorithm is taught that "success" looks like a specific vocabulary style or a certain set of extracurriculars from ten years ago, it risks creating a loop that punishes anyone who doesn't fit that mathematical pattern today.

### The "Black Box" of Personality
The real danger in using AI for education is what's known as the "Black Box" problem. When a human reader rejects an applicant, they can at least try to explain their reasoning. When a neural network assigns a low "personality score," it cannot explain why it felt a student lacked "spark." 

Research from the [Harvard Graduate School of Education](https://mcc.gse.harvard.edu) emphasizes that ethical character and service to others are key to a good application. But can a machine measure "character"? If we allow AI to become the gatekeeper of higher education, we might end up with a process where one AI writes the essay and another AI reads it. In that scenario, the human voice is lost entirely.

### A Path Forward
I believe AI belongs in the back office for tasks like sorting transcripts or verifying data. But it should never be the final judge of someone's character. The "slowness" of a human reading an essay isn't actually a flaw. That slowness is where empathy lives. It is where a reader notices the potential in a student who might not have the highest test scores but has a perspective the world needs.

The future of admissions shouldn't be about choosing between efficiency and ethics. It should be about using these tools to expand access while ensuring that no student’s story is reduced to a statistical probability.
